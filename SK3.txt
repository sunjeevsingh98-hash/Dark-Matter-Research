      <section class="section">
        <h2 class="section-heading">Abstract</h2>
        <p>
          This preprint introduces the <strong>Inverse Mass Law</strong> as a new theoretical framework connecting
          apparent mass, vacuum energy density and space-time curvature. Instead of assuming undiscovered
          dark matter particles, the model treats gravitational mass as an emergent quantity produced by
          geometric variations in the vacuum.
        </p>
        <p>
          A reciprocal relationship between effective mass and vacuum energy distribution is explored, with
          consequences for galactic rotation curves, gravitational lensing, cosmological expansion and local
          mass anomalies. The work aims to provide a compact geometric alternative to particle dark matter,
          and invites critical review, refinement and observational tests.
        </p>
      </section>

          <h2 class="section-heading">Core Idea</h2>
          <h3>What is the Inverse Mass Law?</h3>
          <p>
            In standard cosmology, dark matter is typically modeled as an invisible particle component.
            The Inverse Mass Law instead proposes that what we interpret as additional mass can be encoded
            in how vacuum energy and geometry are distributed.
          </p>
          <p>
            Let <span class="math-inline">œÅ<sub>v</sub></span> denote vacuum energy density and
            <span class="math-inline">m<sub>eff</sub></span> the effective gravitational mass inferred from dynamics.
            The core postulate is that
          </p>
          <div class="math-block">
            m<sub>eff</sub> ‚àù 1 / œÅ<sub>v</sub><br /><br />
            ‚üπ &nbsp; m<sub>eff</sub> = k / œÅ<sub>v</sub> &nbsp; ,<br />
            where k encodes geometric and scale-dependent factors.
          </div>
          <p>
            Regions with lower effective vacuum energy density behave as if they contain more mass, without
            requiring additional unseen particles.
          </p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <h2 class="section-heading">Implications</h2>
